Global Weekly Movements

U.S. Equities

  • S&P 500 Index: 6,279.35 (2.09%)
    • As we enter the second half of the year the SPX closes at a new all-time high following a holiday shortened trading week, with all sectors in the index posting gains. Following better than expected labor data and new trade talks, gains were headed by a 4.09% increase in materials (XLB) and a 2.88% increase in consumer discretionary (XLY). Consumer discretionary stock Nike (NKE) led total gains for the index this week up over 20%.
  • Dow Jones Industrial Average: 44,828.53 (3.04%)
  • Russell 3000 Index: 3,573.18 (2.19%)
  • NASDAQ Composite: 20,601.10 (1.90%)
    • The tech heavy Nasdaq also closed at a new record to start the third quarter, as investors pile into tech stocks and AI leaders. The rally was led by Apple and Nvidia, with Nvidia also hitting an all time high of $160.94. Investors cite strong demand for AI chips and Apple’s AI ecosystem to support their trades, while this helps show increasing confidence in the tech sector's ability to deliver, even in an evolving trade environment.
  • Big Movers of the Week
    • NIKE Inc (NKE): 76.39 (22.15%)
      • Nike shares rise for the second week in a row as the footwear and sportswear industry gets news of Trump's trade deal with Vietnam, an evolving manufacturing country. This deal reinforces Nike’s plan to try and diversify from a China supply chain, with factories in Vietnam producing half of all Nike brand footwear in 2024. The company will continue with its revamp plan as price raises are set to go into place during the fall.
    • First Solar Inc (FSLR): 185.03 (18.33%)
      • First Solar, an American tech company that specializes in solar modules, saw shares rise after Trump’s tax and spending bill turned out to be better for the solar industry than earlier predicted. While the bill still estimates phasing out of key solar tax credits, the timeline for the phaseout has increased and taxes on solar and wind projects were dropped.
    • Centene Corp (CNC): 33.31 (-38.12%)
      • Centene pulled its 2025 guidance as shares dropped 40% on Wednesday to its lowest since 2017. Citing slower market growth and higher morbidity in its Affordable Care Act (ACA) business, analysts are now estimating that 2025 EPS could be halved from initial guidance. Centene also expects to receive $1.8 billion less in revenue from federal risk adjustment programs, along with rising medical costs. Investors reacted harshly, erasing billions in market value on Wednesday.

U.S. Monetary & Fiscal Policy:

Trade Talk: U.S. made significant progress in trade negotiations this week as a deal with Vietnam was finalized and Canada agreed to scrap their digital service tax. In news with Vietnam, the U.S. agreed to take 20% off Vietnamese exports to America, down from the previous 46%, as well as impose a 40% duty on any goods transhipped. Apparel and footwear brands in the U.S. saw shares rise after this news, but this could affect China who has apparently relied on transshipment to bypass trade barriers. Canada also discarded its service tax in an attempt to get trade talks with the U.S. back on the table. Canada is our biggest trading partner with 75% of Canadian exports going to the states. Trump remarked that further trade letters will be sent out this July 4th.

Global Macroeconomic News:

Non-Farm Payrolls & CME Fedwatch: U.S. job growth in June came in stronger than expected, with nonfarm payrolls rising to 147,000 compared to forecasts of 110,000. The unemployment rate also fell to 4.1% against expectations of a slight increase to 4.3%. Although this data did come in well above expectations, government job creations actually made up half of the non-farm payroll data with 73,000 state and local hirings. With this, I would say it is hard to meaningfully conclude that the labor market is indeed incredibly strong. With the Fed's continued warnings of uncertainty resulting from trade and tariffs, expectations for a July rate cut fell over 20% to 4.7%. Investors are forward hoping and look for a cut in September as we await July inflation data.

Sep 17 2025 Meeting:

ADP Private Payrolls: While non-farm payrolls showed a conflicting positive in labor market data, ADP private payrolls recorded -33,000 jobs added in June, dropping for the first time in two years. Expectations for private payrolls was an increase of 100,000 jobs, but came in well below estimates with the biggest drop in service related openings since March of 2020. Service providers showed to be reducing payrolls by 66,000 last month, along with private-sector employees wage growth cooling. Employees who stayed at current jobs saw only a 4.4% increase in pay, while those that switched jobs saw a 6.8% increase in pay. Markets appeared to shrug off this more negative data but the fact remains that services are the bedrock of the U.S. economy. The big question remains; do people have the disposable income to afford services, and if they don't, are they the ones getting laid off?
