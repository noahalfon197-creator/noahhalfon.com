U.S. Monetary & Fiscal Policy:

Labor Market: US hiring slowed last month but not as much as expected. Non farm payrolls rose by 139,000 in May, topping economist estimates of 125,000, with nearly half the jobs coming from healthcare. The unemployment rate has stayed steady at 4.2%. Separately, federal government jobs shrank by 22,000 for the fourth consecutive month as effects from DOGE cost cutting methods continue to drive reductions. Overall, this broader labor data is seen as a modest positive for markets, though signs of slowing growth continue and persistent pressures suggest the Fed may remain hesitant to ease policy too soon.

CME Fedwatch: As the June FOMC meeting approaches, probability of a 25 basis point cut has dropped to 2.6% while the likelihood for a September cut rises to 51.8%. This reflects the Fed’s cautious stance and dilemma as stable labor market data persists and lingering uncertainty around tariffs and economic growth remains. Even as President Trump pressures Chair Jerome Powell to ease policy, the FED has reaffirmed to stay reactionary and take a data driven approach.

US-China Trade Talk: US-China will hold further trade talks in London after China claims the US violated terms of their recent agreements. While tariffs were temporarily rolled back in May, China continues to limit outbound shipments of key materials/rare earths critical for US manufacturing and tech. In response, the U.S. is accelerating efforts to reduce its dependence on Chinese supply, backing domestic mining and processing, but the change will not come overnight. Rare earth minerals are increasingly being viewed not just as commodities, but as strategic assets.

Global Macroeconomic News:

US Trade Deficit: The US trade deficit has declined by the largest amount on record falling from $138  billion in March to $61.6  billion in April. Imports plunged by a record 16.3% resulting from businesses front loading goods ahead of tariffs and importers inability to decide on what to do with inventory, while exports rose a small 3%. Economists say that although these numbers can temporarily boost quarterly GDP, this is not a long term fix for the United States growing fiscal deficit.

European Central Bank (ECB): The ECB cut rates another 25 basis points for the eight time this year lowering their rates to the lowest level since 2023. With the deposit rate now at 2%, the gap between the ECB and the Fed continues to widen, as the ECB President says the central bank is approaching the end of its monetary policy cycle. Both the euro and government bonds rose following this decision, continuing a trend supported by gains in EU defense technology stocks driven by new security requirements across the region.

Passport Denied: President Trump cracks down on immigration as he bans travel from 12 countries entering the U.S. deemed security concerns. More broadly, the recent changes have led to a decline in travel to the U.S., with the World Travel & Tourism Council projecting losses upwards of $12.5 billion. In a related move, the administration has also prohibited foreign students from enrolling at Harvard, continuing an ongoing battle between the administration and academic institutions.
