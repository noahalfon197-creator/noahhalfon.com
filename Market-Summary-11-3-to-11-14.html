U.S. Monetary & Fiscal Policy

Government Shutdown Ends: The US government shutdown has officially ended after a 43 day stalemate, removing a major macro overhang for markets. Federal operations and data releases will now resume despite missing major data for the month of October, but investors expect a rebound in consumer spending from restored federal paychecks and recurrence of economic data. Risk sentiment is getting a short term boost, but markets remain cautious as the underlying budget tensions and fears over no data may halt rate cuts. The White House also appears to be shifting its attention toward the broader issue of affordability in the near future.

CME Fedwatch: Stocks pulled back this week as probabilities of a 25 BPS cut for the December meeting fell from 95% to 44.7%. A lack of government data stemming from the shutdown reveals that there will be no October CPI and jobs data will be without a reading of the unemployment rate. Both unemployment and inflation concerns continue to grow amidst uncertain trade policy and continued layoffs by big US companies. Powell’s huge argument against the December cut was that the labor market has turned out stronger than expected, and with little October data all eyes will be turned towards November's readings.

ADP Private Payrolls: ADP reports US private employers cut an average of 11,250 jobs per week in October, bringing the four week total to -45,000 jobs for the month. This contrasts with ADP’s monthly report, which showed that private payrolls rose by 42,000 for the month ended. Goldman Sachs also published an estimate showing that payrolls fell by around -50,000, which ends up in line with what ADP is now claiming. This would mark the second largest drop since 2020, and possibly show a weakening labor market where wage growth slows amidst major layoffs.

Global Macroeconomic News

Institutions Saving the Day: Global stock rallies appeared to have hit a bump as continuous intraday selloffs and rebalancing occurs. Amid the concerns and selloffs, institutions appeared to be the savior and helped stop the bleeding. Bank of America commented last week that institutional clients were net buyers of US equities with $4.3 billion in ETF inflows, while hedge funds and retail investors were net sellers during the same time. Outflows from tech were highest, and inflows from staples and healthcare were the greatest. Although many indexes like the NASDAQ and SPX are down for the week, this may represent a positive rebalancing where a long term bullish outlook holds true due to:

    • Strong tech earnings and capex to support growth
    • Dovish Fed policy and growth orientated balance sheet

Earlier this week we also saw large selloffs where many tech names went very red, but the intraday SPX only down hundredths of a percent. This helps reinforce a net bullish outlook as it looks like a lot of the money is reflowing into other investments. Bloomberg also remarked that US ETFs saw $40 billion in inflows over just the past 5 days, and have already pulled in $1.16 trillion YTD.

The Big Short 2?: Famous investor Michael Burry has made headlines this past week after calling for an AI bubble and revealing his short outlook for the market. He also deregistered his hedge fund, Scion Asset Management, as of November 10, 2025, and is returning outside capital by year end. His exit comes amid one of his most aggressive bets yet against the AI boom, with Scion holding put options totaling over $1 billion against large tech and AI companies.

AI Bubble: Despite warnings of an AI market bubble, I still see indicators that suggest healthy market trends. One example is AMD was recently upgraded to a $380 price target, reflecting a 35% revenue CAGR over the next 3-5 years. Is 35% growth really net bearish? Also, following the large cap trade around big tech companies, these giants are readily producing important tech where the real return on investment hasn't been fully seen. While debt financing concerns from AI data center buildouts remain one of the major reasons for the AI argument, the money continues to show that companies are aggressively spending to generate a ROI on their increased capex margins. Even among the current pullbacks in stock price, many of these companies are still up 2-3x for the year where a drop in price may actually represent an entry. I guess we will see if companies can prove their huge capex and spending margins before the “bubble” pops.
