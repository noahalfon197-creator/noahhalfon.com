U.S. Monetary & Fiscal Policy

Fed Cuts and Outlook: The Federal Reserve votes 10-2 to cut rates by another 25 bps, now putting the federal funds rate at 3.75-4.0%. Chair Jerome Powell also commented that a rate cut in December is not certain, pushing markets back and forth on this news. The Fed also said it would stop shrinking its portfolio of assets beginning Dec. 1, closing the book on a process that began in 2022. The Fed has since shed more than $2 trillion in Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities, bringing the balance sheet below $6.6 trillion, its smallest size since 2020.

Trump-Xi: President Trump and Chinese leader Xi Jinping finish their in person meeting delivering significant progress in US-China relations. Outcomes included the US agreeing to cut overall tariffs on Chinese goods to 47% from 57% and reduce fentanyl-related tariffs to 10% in exchange for China’s pledge to curb fentanyl exports. Beijing will also resume US soybean purchases and restart rare earth exports, suspending additional RE export controls for one year. Both sides also agreed to keep talking on Nvidia chip restrictions, as well as cooperation regarding Ukraine. Markets rallied modestly on easing geopolitical tensions and company specific sales news.

Global Macroeconomic News

Trade Updates: Outside of US-China talks, the US also agreed on deals with South Korea. The deal cuts US tariffs on Korean autos to 15% from 25%, while South Korea pledges a $350B investment package in US energy, infrastructure, and maritime shipbuilding projects. Seoul also agreed to increase purchases of American LNG, while tightening AI export controls and reducing regulatory measures for tech, pharma, and biotech companies.

Other Asian country deals include; Malaysia and Cambodia reciprocal trade agreements, including Thailand and Malaysia critical minerals cooperation. Projects with Japan aim to advance a $550  billion investment in US industrial sectors, secure U.S. energy purchases, and establish landmark agreements on critical minerals.

US Consumer Confidence: US consumer confidence levels took a hit in October, falling for the third straight month to the lowest level since April. Darkening views of the future stemming from concerns over a weakening labor market and government instability are heavy contributors to these stats. Among the government shutdown, a greater share of consumers now expect fewer jobs available and less income in the next six months.

Big Tech Earnings: Big tech earnings crushed this week as numerous members of the Magnificent 7 topped estimates and blew past analysts expectations. Starting with Google (GOOGL), EPS came in at $2.87 vs $2.27, while revenue came in at $102B, a first $100B quarter. They also saw capex expectations increase from $85B to $93B, indicating they have no plans to slow down AI scaling and data center development. Microsoft (MSFT) also beat expectations, with EPS coming in at $4.13 vs $3.66 and a revenue beat of around $2 million. Amazon’s (AMZN) Q3 also came in extremely bullish, riding AWS growth to 20.2% ahead of 18% estimates. Shares were up 9% on the day. Lastly, Meta (META) had a revenue beat of 26% YoY to $51.2B vs $49.4B, but saw shares fall almost -14% this week after investors saw Q3 spending hit $19.4 billion and 2025 capex guidance rising to $70-72 billion. Management also warned that 2026 spending will climb even higher. Interesting how investors view Meta’s increase as a negative but push Nvidia to a $5 trillion equity value on the idea of higher capex creating value.

Impacted Equities: GOOGL, MSFT, AMZN, META

​​G-7 + Critical Minerals: The G7 formally announced its first round of strategic projects and measures under the Critical Minerals Production Alliance to accelerate the development of secure critical minerals supply chains. Representatives from the US, Canada, UK, Germany, France, Italy, Australia and Japan met in Toronto this weekend to discuss actions to counterweight China's dominance in the sector. Canada took center stage, announcing 26 new investments and partnerships worth $6.4 billion to accelerate mining, refining, and processing capacity. These projects are aimed at deploying private capital (including government funding) to help establish price floors and coordinated stockpiling, ensuring a more stable supply and reducing exposure to Chinese supply disruptions.

One example is Ucore Rare Metals (UURAF, mentioned in previous posts), which was conditionally approved for up to $36.6 million in federal money to fund their facility in Kingston, Ontario to help scale their mineral processing. Funding to expand facilities or to speed up processes directly translates from operation efficiency into revenue generation as projects ramp, turning into margin expansion and driving higher multiples for these companies. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has also remarked this week that the US will be moving at “warp speed” for the next year or two to get out from China’s global rare earth dominance. In other bullish mineral news, India plans to triple its investment in rare earth magnet manufacturing to $788 million.

Long term, I see funding from the DoW, DOE, EXIM, and allied governments accelerating in both size and scale as demand for AI/data centers, EV magnets, and energy infrastructure drive the need for minerals and resilient supply chains. Refiners and processors will become even better positioned to secure financing and offtake agreements, especially if China's timeline for a mineral truce is one year. Even if prices temporarily dip or companies struggle to ramp projects, the long term outlook for these companies remains sound, and access to government and defense contracts is poised to rise. Bullish? I think so.
