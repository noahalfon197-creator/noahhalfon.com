U.S. Monetary & Fiscal Policy:

U.S. Trade Talk: US trade talks continue to be the hot topic as the July 9th deadline for reciprocal tariffs approaches. This week President Trump finalized a deal with China regarding rare earth exports, offering to lift select U.S. countermeasures in exchange for critical material access. Markets reflected this trade optimism as they remained little changed at this week's close despite Trump’s decision to suspend all trade discussions with Canada. Trump cited Canada’s digital services tax as discriminatory toward American tech firms in his explanation of his decision. In other news, the White House withdrew Section 899 “revenge tax” from Trump's bill after reaching a deal with G7 allies to shield U.S. companies from similar foreign digital taxes. Lastly, Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick remarked that 10-20 trade agreements are in advanced stages and details are expected to be finalized in the coming weeks.

Big Beautiful Bill: Trump's new tax bill passes the senate, marking the largest non discretionary spending cut in U.S. history representing a major pivot in fiscal policy. The bill is designed to stabilize spending and up the rate of growth to stabilize the deficit to GDP ratio (currently 6.4%). Despite this growth structure, the bill’s tax cuts are projected to add over $2 trillion to the deficit, pushing U.S. debt past $40 trillion in the next decade. This move has already influenced the bond market, where markets are bracing for more Treasury issuance and higher yields for bonds with longer maturities. This bill may boost economic growth and business confidence into 2026, but the yield curve is expected to steepen and upward pressure on long term rates could continue.

Global Macroeconomic News:

PCE and Core PCE: U.S. PCE, the FED’s preferred inflation gauge, increased 0.1% for the month of May putting core inflation at 2.3%. Core PCE excluding food and energy also increased 0.2% to 2.7%, above expectations of 2.6%. Along with this data, consumer spending fell -0.1% as well as personal spending which dropped -0.4% against the expected 0.3%. This decline in spending is seen from a weakening demand for services like transportation, dining, travel, and accommodations. These developments raise concerns as low household demand for services could spill over into slower job creation, while on the other hand wages continue to rise. Higher wages support consumer purchasing power, but this uneven pattern could affect future inflation readings and delay the Federal Reserve’s decision to cut rates.
