U.S. Monetary & Fiscal Policy:

Fed News: After this week's FOMC meeting the central bank has decided again to keep rates unchanged at 4.25-4.5% while still reducing the FEDs balance sheet. Following the FEDs data driven approach, the decision for no cut was based on the uncertainty of how large and strong expected inflation from tariffs actually is. Chair Jerome Powell stated that while housing and non-housing services inflation are trending toward the Fed’s 2% target, goods inflation is ticking up and that inflation from tariffs will eventually cause someone to pay (usually the end consumer). Despite emerging split views on whether or not the FED should cut, the median dot plot still reflects expectations for two rate cuts in 2025.

Global Macroeconomic News:

Labor Deportation: As deportations continue and immigration tightens down, the U.S. labor market may start to face some struggles. Even among growing protests and outspoken disapproval, President Trump has ordered ICE to expand efforts into Los Angeles, Chicago, and New York. With the loss of thousands of foreign workers, key sectors like agriculture and construction are starting to see notable labor shortages while also noting slowing operations. If trends continue the labor market may start to show some cracks as manufacturers expect rising wage costs and another layer of pressure as tariff related inflation begins to materialize.

US Retail Sales: U.S. retail sales fell -0.9% in May, the second consecutive monthly decline and steeper than the -0.6% forecast as consumers pull back on spending. Coming largely from autos dropping -3.5% and building materials down -2.7%, gasoline sales also fell -2.0%. Restaurant spending declined -0.9%, marking its steepest drop since early 2023. Following an earlier surge in March where businesses and consumers front loaded purchases ahead of tariff risks, that demand appears to be fading. On the brighter side, control group sales (sales excluding autos, gas, food services, and building materials) rose 0.4%. As a key input for GDP, the increase shows that core consumer spending remains stable despite economic uncertainty.

G7 Summary: Canada’s G7 summit was held under newly appointed Canadian Minister Mark Carney, as leaders from around the world planned to talk on tariffs, war, and regulations. President Trump had an early departure amid the escalating Israel-Iran conflict, but had made progress signing a deal with the UK over tariffs not including steel, as well as an ongoing deal between the U.S. and Canada. In other news, G7 officials warned of slowing global growth, predicted at 2.3-2.4% in 2025, and rising risks from tariffs. As the deadline for Liberation Day tariff pause approaches on July 9th, we look forward to seeing what deals can be made and the effect on worldwide inflation.

Central Bank Watch: Major central banks worldwide held interest rates steady this week as policymakers await a potential economic fallout from tariffs, geopolitical tensions, and mixed growth signals. Besides the U.S. Federal Reserve keeping rates steady, the Bank of England (BoE) also decided to hold at 4.25%. This decision appeared to be more divided than expected, with a potential for a cut in August. Meanwhile, the Bank of Japan (BOJ) maintained its benchmark rate of 0.5%, but notably says it will cut its bond buying from ¥400 million to ¥200 million ($1.34 billion) to try and stabilize ongoing volatility. Amidst current global uncertainty, central banks across the world appear to be taking a more cautious, data-dependent path forward.
